Topic: Gender and School Completion Rate

Are there differences between primary school completion rates depending on gender in the LDCs?

And if there are, what do these differences insinuate?

In this topic, we wanted to explore the differences in primary school completion rates between men and women in the Least Developed Countries (LDC). As there are clear correlations between the growing equal rights of women and higher developed countries, looking at the disparities between education rates between men and women would allow us to see if they have a high education completion rate in general or if it’s a country where men have a proportionally higher opportunity to have an education.
In both graphs we have the completion rates for women and men in the Least Developed Countries. Looking strictly at the averages, men were slightly higher at 56.031% vs women at 54.875%. This doesn’t seem very significant at first with a less than 1.5% difference; however, looking closer at the data the standard deviations between the two have a slightly higher variation with men at 16.31 and 21.04 for women. This is explained by the deviation of rates between men and women at the extremes.

The countries like Sao Tome and Principe that have high completion rates for both men and women at 90% and 86% and Myanmar at 84% for men and 78% for women, the countries with the lowest completion rates have a much higher difference between men and women. South Sudan is the lowest for both men and women, but the clear difference is that women's rates are nearly half of that of the men’s at 18% to 30%.
Implications for this is that while the countries with higher rates of education have small differences between men and women’s completion rates, the countries with the lowest rates of education have significantly higher differences. This follows the correlations between low education rates and high education opportunity difference for gender. Ultimately though, amongst the least developed countries there are only significant gender discrepancies amongst the lowest of those countries.

Which country has the Greatest Completion Rate Gap between Urban and Rural Areas?

In this topic, we sought to compare primary school completion rates in rural versus urban areas within each region. In order to properly evaluate these differences, we created a graph calculating the completion rate of urban areas subtracted from that of rural areas. Then, the table encoded the bars displaying these differences within each country with colors indicating the country’s region. The bars were ordered according to the difference value. Clusters of bars along the ordered The bar graph allowed us to investigate whether larger bars were encoded in a majority in color. This would inform us whether countries in a certain region had greater completion rate differences between urban and rural areas. After creating this visualization, it became clear that the SSA region, or Sub-Saharan African region, encompassed the countries with the greatest difference in completion rates between urban and rural areas.

We also created a map displaying the rural versus urban completion rates. The map was encoded with a diverging color scale moving from blue to yellow to red. Countries encoded in blue represented countries with greater urban completion rates than rural areas while red encoded the opposite. Luminance represented the extent of difference. Yellow indicated countries with marginal differences in completion rates between rural and urban areas. With this encoding, the map made it clear that, globally, there were no significant completion rate differences where urban areas were lower than rural areas. By contrast, the map showed there were many significant completion rate differences with urban areas exceeding that or rural areas. This is seen by the amount of blue on the map and lack of red. On top of this, the map supported data displayed in the table by showing a concentration of blue countries in Africa and the greatest disparity occuring in the African country Gabon, with urban exceeding rural completion rates 43.7 more.

This information continues our story of related issues to the primary school completion rate gaps in Least Developed Countries. Since we have concluded that many of the Least Developed Countries are in Africa, we have also concluded in this topic that many countries in the Sub-Saharan African region have the greatest gaps between urban and rural school completion rates. Further, we discovered amongst these gaps, urban areas outperform rural areas in school completion rates while the opposite is almost never true. These great education disparities between urban and rural areas in majority African countries provide insight into not only the overall low primary school completion rates in African Least Developed Countries, but also the issue of distribution and access to education.

In what way is wealth class and school completion rate correlated?
To find a pattern between class and completion rate, a line graph was drawn to detect any possible correlation. Instead of using time as the x-axis variable, I used the five different wealth classes. Even before visualizing this data, it is obvious that the higher the wealth class, the higher the school completion rate. The next question is to ask, how wide is the discrepancy between wealth classes? A line graph visualization was chosen because the slope is an easy visualization to observe. A wider discrepancy is indicated by a drastic slope in the line graph. Flatter lines indicate a narrower discrepancy between completion rates and wealth class. From the visualization, it seemed that the slopes were similar across different countries, indicating that the completion rate discrepancies were also similar. Another comparison could be made between the different countries. For example, although there are discrepancies, there is also a general difference between the countries of all classes. Some countries’ poor classes have higher school completion rates than the richest class of another country. For example, South Sudan’s richest class have a school completion rate of about 45%, while the poorest class of neighboring Sudan have about 49% school completion rate. The range of completion rates can differ drastically depending on the country.
